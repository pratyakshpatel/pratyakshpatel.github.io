Matchday Math

15 July

I wrote this originally for Chrysalis, the science magazine of IISERB, I am a big time football fan, and also love statistics.

"...the ultimate game of our planet", said Peter Drury. But is the unpredictability that makes it so, slipping away?

Football's appeal lies in its chaos — the miracle goals, the last-minute winners, the David vs Goliath moments. But just as cities gentrify, so do leagues. Top-tier clubs continue to dominate, not by accident, but through capital-driven talent accumulation. The more they win, the more money they earn, reinforcing dominance — and predictability.

Quantifying the Edge: Home Advantage

Consider Anfield, the fortress of Liverpool. Statistically, we define score gap as:

\[ x = \text{Home Score} - \text{Away Score} \]

With \( y = 1 \) if the home team wins, and \( y = 0 \) otherwise. A logistic regression can model win probability:

\[ P(\text{win}) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-\mu x + s}} \]

Here, \(\mu\) and \(s\) are learned from historical data using maximum likelihood or least squares methods.

Betting Odds and Real Probability

In betting markets, payouts \( h \) and \( a \) for home/away wins are designed so that:

\[ P_{\text{home}} + P_{\text{away}} < 1 \]

That leftover is the bookmaker's edge. The game isn't just unfair — it's quantifiably tilted.

The Elo Approach

Elo rating systems dynamically adjust team strength based on match outcomes. The expected score \( E_i \) before the match is:

\[ E_i = \frac{1}{1 + 10^{(R_j - R_i)/400}} \]

After the match, update as:

\[ R_i' = R_i + K(s_i - E_i) \]

Here, \( s_i \) is the actual outcome (1 = win, 0 = loss), and \( K \) controls how fast ratings adapt. A logistic model can again be fitted on Elo rating difference to predict outcomes.

Brier Score: Measuring Predictive Accuracy

How good are our models? Enter the Brier score, which measures the mean squared error between predicted probability and actual outcome:

\[ BS = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} (f_i - o_i)^2 \]

Where \( f_i \) is the predicted probability and \( o_i \) is the actual result (0 or 1). In the multi-class case:

\[ BS = \frac{1}{t} \sum_{i=1}^{t} \sum_{j=1}^{r} (f_{ij} - E_{ij})^2 \]

In our binary case (home win vs not), the formula simplifies and gives an average error across all matches — the lower, the better.

Conclusion

If you've made it this far, you're either a stats nerd or a die-hard football fan — possibly both. We walked through the math that tries to capture football’s unpredictability. Yet, no model, no metric can fully explain Leicester City in 2016. That’s the beauty of the game.
